Puzzle role — the strongest methodological feature in Section C
Food neophobia — the reluctance to eat new or unfamiliar foods — is a validated psychological construct (Pliner & Hobden, 1992) that has never been applied to seaweed consumption in a Nigerian context. Including even a 3-item adapted version lets you cite established psychometric literature, compute a neophobia score, and test whether high neophobia predicts lower seaweed acceptance. This is what separates your paper from a basic awareness survey and makes it genuinely methodologically interesting to reviewers.

How the 3 Hypotheses Map to Questions
Hypothesis 1
Education level is significantly associated with awareness of seaweed as a food ingredient.
Independent variable: Q3 (education) · Dependent variable: Q9 (awareness) · Test: Chi-square · Sections used: A + B
Hypothesis 2
Respondents with prior coastal exposure show significantly higher willingness to incorporate seaweed into their diet.
Independent variable: Q7 (coastal exposure) · Dependent variable: Q22 (acceptance) · Test: Chi-square + logistic regression · Sections used: A + D

Introduction
700–900 words▼
Paragraph 1 — Global context
Open with the global rise of seaweed as a food source — FAO projections on blue food, seaweed aquaculture growth, and demand from health-conscious consumers in Asia, Europe, and North America. Cite 3–4 recent papers.
Paragraph 2 — Nutritional and scientific rationale
Summarise the nutritional profile of macroalgae: iodine, dietary fibre, minerals, bioactive polysaccharides, antioxidants. Brief mention of key species (Sargassum, Ulva, Gracilaria) relevant to West Africa. Keep citations tight — this is not a review paper.
Paragraph 3 — West Africa and Nigeria gap
Establish the research gap explicitly. West Africa has significant macroalgae biodiversity (cite WASSD or related papers), yet consumer studies on seaweed as food in the region are virtually absent from the literature. The Lagos context — population size, coastal proximity, growing health-food market — makes it the ideal study setting.
Paragraph 4 — Food neophobia rationale
Briefly introduce food neophobia as a psychological barrier to novel food acceptance. Note it has been applied to insects, novel proteins, and fermented foods but not seaweed in a Nigerian context — this is your methodological contribution.
Paragraph 5 — Aims and objectives
State the three objectives clearly, each as one sentence, directly matching your three hypotheses. End with one sentence on the study's contribution.
Do not write a literature review inside your introduction. Keep it to 5 tight paragraphs. Reviewers can tell when an introduction is padded.

Materials & Methods
900–1,100 words▼
2.1 Study design
Cross-sectional descriptive survey. Justify this design: suitable for capturing population-level attitudes at a single point in time with no intervention required.
2.2 Study area and population
Lagos metropolitan area. Justify Lagos as the study setting — largest urban population in Nigeria (~15 million), strong health-food consumer market, proximity to the Atlantic coast making it directly relevant to seaweed food potential.
2.3 Sampling
Purposive and snowball sampling via online channels (WhatsApp, Twitter/X, LinkedIn). Eligibility: adults ≥18, resident in Lagos, with internet access. Acknowledge the sampling bias this creates (educated, digitally active respondents) and frame it as a study limitation upfront.
2.4 Sample size
Apply Cochran's formula: n = Z²pq/e² = (1.96)²(0.5)(0.5)/(0.05)² = 384. State minimum target of 250 complete valid responses, consistent with similar consumer studies in sub-Saharan Africa (cite 2 examples).
2.5 Instrument development
34-item questionnaire across 6 sections. Likert items adapted from validated instruments (cite Food Neophobia Scale — Pliner & Hobden, 1992; and a food perception scale). Pilot tested with n=30; Cronbach's α = [value] for Q16 subscale, [value] for Q17 subscale.
2.6 Data collection
Google Forms distributed [Month Year] to [Month Year]. Anonymous, voluntary, no incentive.
2.7 Analysis
Frequencies, percentages, means, SD. Chi-square for H1 and H2. Binary logistic regression for H3 (Q22 as outcome). Cronbach's α for internal consistency. JASP v0.18 (free and citable) or SPSS v27. p < 0.05.
2.8 Ethics
Informed consent obtained digitally. No personal data collected. Conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki.

4
Discussion
1,400–1,700 words▼
Opening — headline finding
State the single most important finding in plain language. Immediately begin comparing it to literature — not summarising results again.
Awareness discussion
Compare your awareness rate to similar studies in Ghana, South Africa, or Asian developing countries. Discuss information pathways — why social media dominates over healthcare channels. Discuss the awareness-to-consumption gap and what maintains it.
Perception and neophobia
Interpret the perception composite score. Discuss safety concern (Q21) in the specific context of Lagos coastal water quality — this is your Nigeria-specific contribution that no global study captures. Contextualise neophobia scores against published FNS data from other populations.
Acceptance predictors
Interpret the regression. Discuss each significant predictor's effect size and practical meaning. If coastal exposure (Q7) predicts acceptance — explain the mechanism (familiarity, cultural normalisation). If neophobia predicts non-acceptance — this is a novel finding worth highlighting.
Preferred formats — practical insight
Discuss which food formats Lagos consumers prefer. Frame this as a market entry recommendation. If soup powder or seasoning dominates, argue that integrating seaweed into familiar Nigerian cooking formats (not sushi) is the right product strategy.
Limitations
Online convenience sampling, educated respondent bias, cross-sectional design, single-city scope, hypothetical (not actual) acceptance. Each limitation in one sentence with what it means for interpretation.
The discussion should feel like a conversation with the literature, not a summary of your results. Every paragraph should cite at least one external paper for comparison.
5
Conclusion
200–300 words▼
Three paragraphs only. First: restate what the study found without numbers — the narrative headline. Second: the most important practical implication, framed for industry, researchers, or policy actors. Third: one sentence on the need for follow-up studies (experimental tasting studies, multi-city surveys, economic feasibility of seaweed food production in Nigeria).
Your conclusion's final sentence should be quotable — something a reader could lift and cite. Example: "This study provides the first evidence-based consumer baseline for macroalgae food acceptance in Nigeria, establishing the groundwork for targeted product development and public health nutrition campaigns in the West African blue food sector."

Journal Targets & Submission Strategy
▼
First choice — Foods (MDPI)
Open access, Scopus + Web of Science indexed, IF ~5.2. Publishes consumer perception and novel food acceptance surveys regularly. Fast turnaround (~6–8 weeks). Highly receptive to developing-country context papers. Submit here first.
Second choice — Food Quality & Preference (Elsevier)
The premier journal for consumer food behaviour research. Your food neophobia angle fits perfectly with this journal's scope. More competitive but the methodological sophistication of your regression model makes it viable.
Third choice — Algal Research (Elsevier)
Strong fit given the macroalgae focus. The journal publishes applied socioeconomic and consumer studies alongside lab science. Your NIOMR affiliation and WASSD connection are directly relevant here.
Backup — Heliyon (Elsevier)
Broad open-access journal, peer-reviewed and indexed. Lower barrier. Use only if first three reject.
Conference target
IOCMSE 2026 — submit a separate abstract on the consumer survey results alongside your WASSD abstract. Two submissions from related work at the same conference significantly raises your profile.
Submit to one journal at a time. Use each rejection's reviewer comments to strengthen the paper before the next submission. A paper that goes Foods → Food Quality & Preference → Algal Research with iterative revision will almost always find a home by the third attempt.
